Coaching Nurture Sequence

NursePreneurs

Brand & Audience NursePreneurs coaches nurses who want to leave shift work and build their own practice. The audience has spent years thinking about starting a business, bought courses, but still feels stuck and unsupported.
Strategy This audience had already failed with courses, so any hint of a pitch too early would kill trust instantly. Email 1 names the exact frustration they cannot articulate (the WHAT vs HOW gap). Email 2 lets a real client story do the selling. The offer only appears after the reader has already thought "that sounds like me", so it feels like a logical next step, not a push.

Email 01

Problem Validation, Why Courses Fail

+
SubjectCourses teach WHAT to do. Not HOW to actually do it PreviewThat's why you're stuck with a notebook full of ideas and zero clients...
You've been thinking about starting your nurse business for more than 3 years. You've bought 2, maybe 3 courses. You've joined Facebook groups and mapped out ideas on napkins. But you're still here, working 60-80 hours while getting paid for 40. You're still missing family time and you're still stuck. And at some point, you start wondering: What's wrong with me? Nothing is wrong with you. I know because I've talked to hundreds of nurses exactly like you. They're smart, capable people with years of expertise. The problem wasn't them. It was what they were trying to use. The WHAT vs HOW gap Those courses you bought taught you WHAT to do. They told you to build a website, create an offer, find your niche, and set your prices. You learned all of it. But when you sat down to actually do it, you froze. Because knowing WHAT to do isn't the same as knowing HOW to do it. It's like reading a recipe versus actually cooking the meal. Or watching a surgery versus performing it yourself. That's the gap. That's why you're sitting there with a notebook full of ideas and zero clients. When I ask nurses why their courses didn't work, I hear the same thing over and over: "The information was great. But when I sat down to actually start, I didn't know where to begin." - Nurse entrepreneur, 6 years in ICU "I got stuck and had no one to ask." - Travel nurse, 12 years experience "Week 3, life got busy, and I just stopped." - NICU nurse, 8 years in Does that sound familiar? Information doesn't create transformation. You don't have a knowledge problem. You have an implementation problem. And you can't implement it alone. That's what courses don't give you. Tomorrow I'm telling you about Lori, a DNP with all the credentials and knowledge, but she was stuck working night shifts that were draining her. Then she tried something different. Talk soon, Catie P.S. It wasn't her fault. And it's not yours.

Email 03

Practical Objection-Handling Tips

+
Subject A"Let me check my diary and get back to you" Subject BThey say no to a meeting. Now what? Subject CWhat to say when they won't book?
Hey [Name], Gilly here. Remember BAMFAM from the Playbook? Book A Meeting From A Meeting. Simple idea. But then you try it and hear: "Let me check my diary and get back to you." "Just send me an email." "I'm slammed next week." And suddenly you're back to chasing. Ugh. When they say "Let me check my diary and get back to you": "No worries, why don't we pencil something in now and if it doesn't work, we can move it? How's Tuesday or Wednesday looking?" When they say "Just send me an email": "Happy to. But these things get buried in inboxes. Easier to lock in 15 minutes now. What works better, mornings or afternoons?" When they say "I'm really busy next week": "Totally get it. How about the week after? I could do [day] or [day], would either work?" See the pattern? You're not pushing. You're just making it easy for them to say yes. Try one of these next time and see what happens. Gilly x

Email 04

Practical Guidance on Follow-up Boundaries

+
Subject AWhen to stop following up Subject BSometimes you have to let go Subject CThe email that ends the chase
Hey [Name], Gilly here. We've talked about following up more. But there's another side to this. Sometimes you have to stop. Not every lead is going to buy. Some conversations just don't turn into clients. And at some point, chasing becomes a waste of your energy. My rule: after 5 or 6 follow-ups with no response, it's time. But don't just disappear. Send one last email. Something like: "Hey [Name], I've reached out a few times and haven't heard back, so I'm guessing now isn't the right time. No worries at all. If things change, you know where to find me." Short and respectful. No guilt trips. Funny thing is, this email often gets a reply when nothing else did. People respect it when you give them space. And if they don't reply? You move on knowing you did everything right. Gilly x P.S. I share a lot on social media too, stuff that doesn't always make it into emails. Cart Abandonment Sequence

Brake Free

Brand & Audience Brake Free makes a wireless helmet-mounted brake light for motorcyclists. The audience showed purchase intent but didn't complete checkout, likely held back by hesitation around fit, shipping, or overall value.
Strategy Most cart abandonment emails lead with the discount. I didn't. For motorcycle riders, road visibility isn't a feature, it's tied to a real fear of being unseen. Leading with that emotion first makes the $20 off feel like a bonus rather than the reason to buy. Each email removes a specific layer of hesitation: email 1 reframes the product as protection, email 2 handles logistics with urgency, email 3 answers the technical questions that stop cautious buyers.
